5 Facts about ASD and Web Accessibility

  • One in 36 children, or over 2.7% of 8-year-old children, in the U.S. was diagnosed with ASD in 2020, increasing from 2018 when the estimate was 1 in 44. In 2020, 89.8% of people in America were active internet users. This means that about 2.4%, or about 1 in 41 people, are autistic and use the internet in the U.S.

  • Relatively few users (25%) had ever tried technology designed for people with cognitive disabilities; and only 7% of respondents had used technology products designed specifically for autism in one study.
  • Sensory abnormalities were prevalent in ASD, with 94.4% of the ASD sample reporting extreme levels of sensory processing on at least one sensory quadrant of the AASP in one study.
  • Reading comprehension is an area of difficulty for many individuals with ASD. Word recognition, oral language and social impairments may constrain reading comprehension in ASD.
  • Based on theories and past research, predictive impairment could be a shared characteristic among individuals with autism.

Why is it important to consider people with ASD in online experiences?

1 in 41 people, or about 2.4% of people are autistic and are active internet users in the U.S. Considering the population of the U.S, this means there are millions of active internet users who are also autistic. And, this is only considering people with ASD who live in the U.S; there are millions more people worldwide who have ASD and are active users of the internet.

Tim Berners-Lee, the man known as the inventor of the internet, once said: "The power of the web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect."

As developers and designers who create websites, we need to make websites that are accessible to everyone. People with ASD have the right to access and engage with online content just like any other user. If the way we design a site is barring them from doing so, it's on us to make sure that they can access and use our site. It's necessary to design sites so that everyone has an equal opportunity to access information, services, and resources.

Tips on how to interact with people with ASD through websites and website design

First, one tip as a foreword.

We need to remember that everyone with ASD is different since ASD affects everyone differently. ASD is a spectrum in many ways. For example, some people might struggle with communication‚Äì an estimated 25-30% of individuals with ASD are nonverbal or minimally verbal. Others might have no trouble with talking. So, while the following points might address something that significantly affects some people with ASD, no one point can encompass the needs of everyone with ASD. Accessibility is not a feature; it's a requirement. And, adding one component that might help some people with ASD won't necessarily help all.

Tips on website design that can help us get our content across clearer to individuals with ASD

First, provide instructions or help guides. Break down complex tasks into manageable steps and use visual aids or multimedia elements to reinforce understanding. Providing explicit instructions can help people with ASD navigate the website more independently. Provide options to get help when necessary.

Next, use simple and straightforward language in the website's content. Avoid jargon, complex sentence structures, and ambiguous wording. Avoid using idioms and be straight to the point with what you want to convey in the text. Because reading comprehension can be an area of difficulty for people with ASD, we need to structure the content in a way that's as clear as possible for them to understand.

Finally, use clear, concise instructions on buttons. It can be helpful for there to be visual cues in buttons as well, as long as the visual cue is clear and is accompanied with supporting text. It should be clear what every element on a page does. For example, there shouldn't be buttons with just icons that explain what they do. Because predictive impairment can be a shared characteristic among individuals with ASD, it might not be clear to them what a button or element does when they interact with it unless that's specifically stated on the element.

What about communicating directly, whether that be through emails or phone calls?

Even if we aren't the ones writing the content for the sites that we build, we might have website users with ASD who want to communicate directly with us, whether that be for feedback purposes or questions about the content on the site. We can make this easier by having a clear section on the site to take feedback from users. Here's one tip on how to better communicate through email with those with ASD.

Use straightforward language when communicating, whether that be through emails or phone calls. Avoid excessive jargon, metaphors, or ambiguous phrases that could be confusing or misinterpreted. If you're writing an email, organize it using clear headings, bullet points, or numbered lists. If the email involves scheduling a follow-up, provide clear details about the time, location, and any specific agenda items.

Tips on how to create more accessible websites for people with ASD

When designing an online product, we need to take into account sensory sensitivities that individuals with ASD may experience by avoiding the use of excessive audio, flashing lights, or bright colors that could be overstimulating. It would also be helpful to provide options to adjust or mute audio, control visual effects and colors, or manage sensory input in other ways. For example, the site could have options for users to take sensory breaks, pause or save progress, and return to the site when ready. This abides by UDL's guidelines to provide options for self regulation by facilitating pausing if necessary.

It would also be helpful if the site offers customizable features that allow individuals with ASD to tailor the online experience to their specific needs. This might include options to adjust font sizes, color schemes, sound settings, and line spacing. Some individuals with ASD may be sensitive to certain sounds or colors. Offering the ability to adjust the website's features and design can create a more comfortable browsing experience for them. This feature would go with UDL's guidelines to provide options for perception by offering multiple ways to customize the site's features.

Next, we should incorporate visuals on the site. This can include things like images or icons to supplement the content of the site and the elements on the page. These visuals can help individuals with ASD comprehend information, follow instructions, and understand what happens when interacting with certain parts of the site. This would go with UDL's guidelines to provide options for expression and communication by adding multiple ways to view information.

The overall design of the site should be uncluttered and organized; the site needs to minimize distractions and provide a clear focus. We can do this by using a consistent layout, simple navigation, and avoiding excessive visual stimuli. Elements like pop-up windows should be avoided. The more predictable and clear the website design is, the easier it would be for individuals with ASD to navigate it. The default design of the site should be clear by using sans-serif font faces or other clear typefaces.

Finally, the site must maintain predictability and consistency in the way it's designed. Individuals with ASD often thrive in familiar environments where things are predictable. So, we need consistency in our website design for a better user experience. Specifically, for example, we can keep the nav bar on top and the footer on the bottom. If there are elements that show up after something gets clicked on the site, we can use visual indicators like progress bars to show what's happening. A success message or error notification can pop up at the end as well to make the flow of an individual's actions and the system's response clearer.

Additional Resources

Here are some additional resources that can be helpful when making your own website to be more accesible.

  • It might be useful to listen to an episode of the Accessibility Rules podcast, where Nic Steenhout interviews people that are involved with web accessibility. In particular, this interview with Jamie Knight, an autistic web developer, is very helpful for designing web pages for autistic people. It's interesting to listen to Knight's views on online accessibility because of their perspective as an autistic person. They talk about how they became a web developer, the principles they keep in mind as they create sites, and their experience as an autistic person in web development.
  • Here's a GitHub repository with code that generates colors that are friendly for people with ASD. A study by GA Architects, a group of architects specialized in building sensory-friendly environments for children and adults with ASD, it was found that subdued and colors mixed with gray were favored by children with autism, there was a preference for colors in the blue/green hue sectors, and a balance between colourfulness and grayness was seen to be popular. This open-source color generator takes those points into account in order to generate colors that are less likely to cause sensory overstimulation.
  • This is a color contrast checker tool that you can use to check if the text and elements on your site are contrasted enough for them to be accessible. This site was developed by WebAIM, the Institute for Disability Research, Policy, and Practice at Utah State University. This tool checks the colors of the background against the text colors in order to see if they abide by the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). This resource can help prevent sensory overload or underload which individuals with ASD might experience.
